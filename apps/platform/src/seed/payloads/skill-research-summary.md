# Research Summary

Follow this sequence whenever a teammate drops a long source on your desk and
asks for a digest.

1. **Skim-read for the spine.** Read the title, abstract (or opening
   paragraph), every heading, and the conclusion. Write a one-sentence
   hypothesis about the source's main claim before reading body text — you
   will revise it.
2. **Extract claims with evidence anchors.** Open a working note and list each
   distinct claim the source makes. For every claim, record a short quote or
   page/section reference so a reader can verify. Flag any claim with no
   supporting evidence as `UNSUPPORTED`.
3. **Bucket into themes.** Cluster the claims into 3–5 themes. Name each
   theme in four words or fewer. Drop any claim that does not fit a theme —
   leftovers become the "Open Questions" section.
4. **Draft the summary.** Write one paragraph per theme, leading with the
   strongest claim and citing the source once per paragraph. Avoid the words
   "interesting", "insightful", or "valuable" — state what the source says,
   not how it felt.
5. **Close with Next Steps.** Give the requester two or three concrete
   follow-ups: questions to ask, experiments to run, or documents to pull.

Keep the final output under 400 words unless the requester asks for more.
